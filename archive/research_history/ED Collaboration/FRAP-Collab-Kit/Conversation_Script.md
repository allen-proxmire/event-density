# Conversation Script: First Meeting with a Potential Collaborator

## Before the Meeting

- Have the One-Page Pitch printed or on screen.
- Know their name, lab, and one recent paper they published.
- Be ready to explain the experiment in under 60 seconds without jargon.

---

## 60-Second Overview

*Use this as your opening after introductions:*

"I have a quantitative prediction for how diffusion works at high protein concentration. Standard theory says a photobleached spot in a concentrated BSA solution should recover with a specific speed — the square root of time. My theory says it should be about three times slower. A single afternoon on a confocal microscope with FRAP can tell us which is right. I provide the complete protocol and do all the analysis. I am looking for someone who can run the microscope."

---

## Three Key Points to Convey

1. **The experiment is standard FRAP.** Nothing exotic. Standard confocal, standard fluorescent protein, standard bleach-and-watch. The only unusual thing is the high protein concentration (200-350 mg/mL).

2. **The prediction is sharp and falsifiable.** We predict an exponent of 0.16; standard theory predicts 0.50. These are easy to distinguish. The result is clear-cut either way — no ambiguous middle ground.

3. **The time commitment is minimal.** One afternoon of imaging. We handle everything else: protocol design, data analysis, interpretation, manuscript preparation.

---

## Three Questions to Ask Them

1. **"Does your confocal have a FRAP module, and have you used it recently?"**
   *(Confirms capability and availability.)*

2. **"Have you worked with concentrated protein solutions before — say, above 100 mg/mL?"**
   *(Gauges their comfort level. If they say yes, they are ideal. If no, it is still feasible but they may need sample-prep guidance.)*

3. **"What is the typical turnaround time to get on the microscope?"**
   *(Establishes timeline. If the answer is "next week," great. If "three months," consider alternatives.)*

---

## How to Handle Skepticism

### "Why should the recovery be slower? That sounds wrong."

"At high concentration, the protein molecules are so tightly packed that diffusion almost stops. The mobility — the rate at which molecules can move past each other — vanishes as a power law near the packing limit. This makes the concentration front propagate much more slowly than you would expect from the average diffusion coefficient. It is the same physics as the porous medium equation in fluid mechanics — slow fronts with sharp edges."

### "What if the result is negative?"

"Then we have cleanly falsified a specific prediction, which is publishable and scientifically valuable. I am not asking you to confirm my theory — I am asking you to test it. Either outcome advances the science."

### "I do not know anything about your theory."

"You do not need to. The experiment is a standard FRAP measurement. The only thing that is different is that I have a specific number to compare the result against. You run the microscope; I handle the theory."

### "We are too busy right now."

"I completely understand. Could I follow up in [a month / after the semester / after your grant deadline]? This is not time-sensitive — the prediction will still be there."

---

## How to Close the Conversation

If they are interested:
"Great. I will send you the one-page summary and the detailed protocol by email. If you have any questions after reading it, I am happy to discuss. The next step would be a pilot FRAP at one concentration to make sure the setup works."

If they are uncertain:
"I appreciate you hearing me out. Let me send you the one-page summary so you can think about it. No pressure — just let me know if it seems like a fit."

If they are not interested:
"Thank you for your time. If you know anyone in your department who does FRAP on concentrated solutions, I would appreciate an introduction."
