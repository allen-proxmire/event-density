# Lab Identification Guide: Finding the Right FRAP Collaborator

## Ideal Lab Profile

The experiment requires exactly one piece of equipment (a confocal microscope with FRAP module) and one skill (preparing concentrated protein solutions). The ideal collaborator has:

### Must-Have

- **Confocal microscope with FRAP capability.** Any major brand (Zeiss LSM, Leica SP, Nikon A1, Olympus FV). The FRAP module must support user-defined ROI bleaching and immediate post-bleach time-lapse acquisition.
- **488 nm laser line.** Standard on all confocals (for FITC/Alexa488 excitation).
- **Temperature-controlled stage.** Heated insert or incubation chamber, stable to +/-0.5C.
- **Willingness to share raw image data.** We need TIFF stacks, not processed curves.

### Nice-to-Have

- **Experience with FRAP on proteins or colloids.** Not required — our protocol is self-contained — but it reduces setup time.
- **Access to BSA-FITC.** Commercially available (Sigma, ThermoFisher). If not in stock, we can provide it.
- **High frame rate capability.** 30-60 fps in confocal mode. Most modern confocals achieve this in a small ROI.

## Types of Labs to Approach

### Tier 1: Best fit

- **Biophysics labs** studying protein dynamics, crowding, or intracellular diffusion. They already do FRAP at high protein concentration. The experiment is routine for them.
- **Soft matter labs** studying colloidal glasses, gels, or dense suspensions. They understand nonlinear diffusion and will appreciate the theoretical context.

### Tier 2: Good fit

- **Cell biology labs** with confocal FRAP expertise. They typically do FRAP on cells, but the same microscope and skills apply to in-vitro protein solutions. The sample is simpler than their usual work.
- **University imaging cores** with a FRAP-capable confocal. A core facility manager who runs the microscope for users can do this as a side project or demonstration.

### Tier 3: Possible but less ideal

- **Chemistry/chemical engineering labs** with DLS or NMR but no confocal. They have the protein expertise but not the microscope. Would need to borrow or access a confocal elsewhere.
- **Physics labs** with custom-built optical setups. They could adapt their system for FRAP but would need to invest time in calibration.

## Red Flags (Labs to Avoid)

- **No confocal microscope.** Widefield FRAP is possible in principle but much harder to control. Confocal is strongly preferred.
- **Only does FRAP on live cells.** They may not want to spend instrument time on an in-vitro sample. Check willingness before investing effort.
- **Proprietary data policies.** If the lab or core facility restricts sharing of raw image data, the collaboration will not work. We need the TIFFs.
- **Very busy instrument with long wait times.** The experiment takes one afternoon, but if the microscope is booked 6 months out, the timeline does not work.

## How to Find Candidates

1. **Search PubMed** for recent papers using "FRAP" + "protein" + "concentrated" or "crowded." Authors of these papers have exactly the right setup.
2. **Check university imaging core websites.** Look for "FRAP" in their list of available techniques. Contact the core manager directly.
3. **Conference contacts.** Biophysical Society, ACS Colloid, Soft Matter Gordon Conference. Ask during poster sessions: "Do you have a confocal with FRAP?"
4. **Local contacts.** A nearby university with a biophysics or bioengineering department is the lowest-friction option.
