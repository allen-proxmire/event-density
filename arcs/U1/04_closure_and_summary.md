## 6. Public-Facing Explainer

*This section is intended for a general scientific audience and is not part of the formal derivation.*

### 6.1 What the participation measure is

In standard quantum mechanics, a particle (or any quantum system) is described by a *wavefunction* — a complex-valued function $\psi(x)$ that assigns a complex number to each point in space. Squaring the magnitude gives the probability density: $|\psi(x)|^2$ is the probability of finding the particle at $x$. Phase content encodes interference. The Schrödinger equation governs evolution. This is the textbook setup, and it works in every quantum experiment ever performed.

But notice: the wavefunction's structure is *given*. It is complex-valued because complex numbers make the math work. It has a magnitude *squared* (not cubed, not absolute value) because $|\psi|^2$ matches experiment. It has phase content because interference exists. None of this is derived; it is the framework you are handed when you learn quantum mechanics.

The Event Density framework wants to derive the wavefunction's structure from a deeper ontology. The deeper object is the *participation measure*:

$$P_K(x, t) = \sqrt{b_K(x, t)} \cdot e^{i \pi(K, x, t)}$$

This says: at every point in spacetime, for every channel $K$ (a primitive structural pathway in the participation graph), there is a complex number whose magnitude is the square root of bandwidth (the local measure of how strongly the system participates) and whose phase is the polarity angle (the direction of the local "tension" in the participation field).

The U1 result of this paper is the structural derivation of *every piece* of this construction — the complex-valued range, the polar decomposition, the squared-magnitude relation, the phase-equals-polarity identification — from ED's primitive stack alone, with no postulates beyond the primitives and standard mathematics.

### 6.2 Why complex numbers and not real or quaternionic

The first question: why does the participation measure have to be complex-valued? Why not real, with phase content stored separately? Why not quaternionic, with richer angular structure?

The argument has two pieces.

First: the polarity phase supplied by Primitive 09 is a $U(1)$ object — an angle on the unit circle, with the rotational symmetry of a circle. It is *not* a sphere, *not* a higher-dimensional angular structure. ED's primitive stack commits to circular polarity, not spherical or higher.

Second: a real-valued carrier cannot faithfully hold a $U(1)$ phase. Real-number-multiplication has a one-dimensional symmetry group (positive scaling, plus possibly sign flip), which is much smaller than $U(1)$. The only ways to act with $U(1)$ on real numbers are trivial ones — multiplying by 1, or by ±1. The phase content of polarity is lost.

You could, in principle, store the data as a *pair* $(r, \theta)$ — a real number and a separate angle. But this construction is mathematically equivalent to a complex number via the polar form $r \cdot e^{i\theta}$. So "real-valued plus separate phase" is just complex-valued under a different name. There is no genuinely distinct alternative.

Quaternions are the next candidate. A quaternionic-valued participation measure would have a richer angular structure: $\mathrm{SU}(2)$, the rotation group of three-dimensional space, embedded as the unit quaternions. But this is *too much* angular content for what Primitive 09 supplies. Primitive 09 gives us $U(1)$, a one-dimensional rotation group. Quaternions give us $\mathrm{SU}(2)$, a three-dimensional rotation group. The extra two dimensions are not supported by any primitive.

You could try to embed $U(1)$ inside $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ — pick an axis in three-dimensional space and let polarity rotate around it. But which axis? Any direction in three-space gives a valid $U(1)$ subgroup. There is a whole sphere's worth of choices, and the primitives provide no basis for selecting one. The carrier becomes structurally underdetermined.

Alternatively, you could use the full $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ structure. But then the participation measure carries angular information beyond what Primitive 09 supplies. Where does that extra structure come from? Not from any primitive. Either you postulate it (which violates the primitive-only methodology), or you have unphysical content in your description (which is structurally incoherent).

The mathematical theorem due to Frobenius (1878) tells us there are no other choices: the only finite-dimensional associative division algebras over the real numbers are $\mathbb{R}, \mathbb{C}, \mathbb{H}$. With $\mathbb{R}$ ruled out (can't hold the phase) and $\mathbb{H}$ ruled out (too much extra structure), $\mathbb{C}$ is the unique surviving option.

The complex numbers are not chosen because they are familiar, or because they make the math elegant. They are *forced* by the matching between primitive content and carrier content.

### 6.3 Why the magnitude must be squared

The second question: why is the bandwidth equal to the *squared* magnitude of the participation measure, rather than the magnitude itself or some other power?

This is the famous "why $|\psi|^2$?" question of quantum mechanics, which the Born rule has stated as a postulate for a hundred years and which Gleason's theorem partially derived from non-contextuality assumptions.

ED's answer comes from one level deeper. Consider what bandwidth has to be. Three structural facts:

- **Bandwidth is a non-negative real number.** This is what Primitive 04 says directly.
- **Bandwidth doesn't depend on global phase.** Rotating the participation measure $P \to e^{i\alpha} P$ shouldn't change the bandwidth — the rotation is an unphysical relabeling of the polarity angle.
- **Bandwidth adds across disjoint channels.** If a chain has access to two distinct primitive channels in the participation graph, the total bandwidth is the sum of the bandwidth in each.

The first two facts together say: bandwidth is some function of $|P|^2$ (the squared magnitude is the unique $U(1)$-invariant quantity on $\mathbb{C}$ up to monotone reparametrization). Call this function $f$, so $b = f(|P|^2)$.

The third fact then constrains $f$. When two disjoint channels combine, the magnitudes squared add (this is the Pythagorean structure of orthogonal vectors), and bandwidth must add too. So $f(a + b) = f(a) + f(b)$ for all non-negative $a, b$.

This is a famous mathematical functional equation called the *Cauchy functional equation*. Its only continuous non-negative solutions are linear: $f(x) = c \cdot x$ for some constant $c$. Setting $c = 1$ by convention, $b = |P|^2$.

Any other power $|P|^\alpha$ with $\alpha \neq 2$ fails the additivity constraint. The function $x \mapsto x^{\alpha/2}$ is super-additive for $\alpha > 2$ (the combined channel would have *more* bandwidth than the sum of its parts) or sub-additive for $\alpha < 2$ (less bandwidth than the sum). Equality holds only when $\alpha/2 = 1$, i.e., $\alpha = 2$.

The squared magnitude is forced by the structural requirement that bandwidth be $U(1)$-invariant *and* additive across disjoint channels. Both facts are primitive-level (Primitive 09 invariance, Primitive 04 additivity). Neither requires Born, Schrödinger, or any downstream theorem.

The Exponent-2 Thread that runs through standard quantum mechanics — squared amplitudes in the Born rule, momentum-squared in the Schrödinger kinetic term, squared variances in Heisenberg's bound — has its origin here: in the Cauchy functional equation applied to $U(1)$-invariant non-negative additive functions on the complex plane.

### 6.4 Why the phase must be the polarity

The third question: why is the phase factor in the participation measure exactly the polarity phase, rather than some function of polarity or some unrelated angular variable?

The answer is short: it has to be polarity because polarity is the only $U(1)$-valued angular variable in the entire ED primitive stack. We checked all 13 primitives. Twelve of them supply real-valued or set-valued or relational structures, with no angular content. Only Primitive 09 supplies a $U(1)$-valued phase.

If you tried to use a *function* of polarity — say, twice the polarity, or polarity plus some real-valued quantity — you would need that function to be primitive-level supported.
