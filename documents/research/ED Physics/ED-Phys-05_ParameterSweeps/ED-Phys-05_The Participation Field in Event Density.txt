## Abstract

The Event Density (ED) architecture contains a global participation field $v(t)$ whose physical interpretation has remained the single largest open question in the framework. This paper resolves the interpretation and derives its consequences. We show that $v(t)$ corresponds to the spatially-averaged Madelung velocity (Ehrenfest mean current) in the quantum regime, generalised with dissipation. Requiring causal propagation uniquely fixes the participation coupling $H$ to the Compton frequency, giving a telegraph propagation speed equal to $c$. We trace $v(t)$ across five physical regimes, identify which interpretations are exact, anchored, or candidate, and derive five falsifiable predictions that distinguish ED from standard physics. Five numerical experiments are proposed to isolate $v(t)$'s effects within the existing ED-SIM-02 simulation engine.

## 2. The $v(t)$ Equation and Its Structural Role

### 2.1 The Coupled System

The ED canonical PDE is a coupled system of one PDE and one ODE:

$$\partial_t \rho = \underbrace{D\,\nabla\!\cdot\!(M(\rho)\nabla\rho)}_{\text{mobility}} - \underbrace{D\,P(\rho)}_{\text{penalty}} + \underbrace{H\,v(t)}_{\text{participation}}$$

$$\dot{v} = -\gamma\,v + H\,\bar{F}[\rho]$$

where:

- $M(\rho) = M_0(\rho_{\max} - \rho)^\beta$ is the degenerate mobility
- $P(\rho) = P_0(\rho - \rho^*)$ is the penalty
- $\bar{F}[\rho] = \langle D[\nabla\!\cdot\!(M(\rho)\nabla\rho) - P(\rho)] \rangle$ is the volume-averaged local forcing
- $H$ is the participation coupling strength
- $\gamma$ is the damping rate

The forcing $\bar{F}[\rho]$ is the spatial mean of everything the density field does *without* participation — the average of the mobility and penalty channels.

### 2.2 Why $v(t)$ Is Global

The participation field $v(t)$ has no spatial index. It is a scalar function of time only. This is not an approximation or a simplification — it is a structural feature of the ED architecture. Whatever $v(t)$ represents physically, it must be an aggregate property of the entire system.

### 2.3 The Feedback Structure

The coupling between $\rho$ and $v$ is bidirectional:

1. **$v(t)$ is driven by the density dynamics.** The forcing $H\bar{F}$ means $v(t)$ responds to what $\rho$ does on average. It does not have independent dynamics.

2. **$v(t)$ feeds back uniformly.** The $+Hv$ term in the $\rho$-equation is the same at every spatial point. This is a mean-field coupling, not a spatially structured interaction.

3. **The combined system is telegraph.** In the spatially uniform limit ($\nabla\rho = 0$), defining $\delta = \langle\rho\rangle - \rho^*$, the coupled system reduces to:

$$\ddot{\delta} + (D P_0 + \gamma)\dot{\delta} + (D^2 P_0^2 + H^2 D P_0)\,\delta = 0$$

This is the telegraph/RLC equation with damping rate $(D P_0 + \gamma)/2$ and natural frequency $\omega_0^2 = D^2 P_0^2 + H^2 D P_0$.

### 2.4 Three Constraints on Any Interpretation

Any physical interpretation of $v(t)$ must satisfy:

| # | Constraint | Reason |
|---|-----------|--------|
| 1 | $v(t)$ must be global (not spatially varying) | It is a scalar ODE, not a PDE |
| 2 | $v(t)$ must be driven by density dynamics | The $\bar{F}[\rho]$ forcing is structural |
| 3 | $v(t)$ must produce telegraph behaviour when coupled to $\rho$ | This is the defining feature of the participation channel |

## 3. The Madelung Mapping

### 3.1 The Standard Madelung Picture

Write $\psi = \sqrt{\rho}\,e^{iS/\hbar}$. The Schrödinger equation splits into two real equations:

**Continuity equation:**

$$\partial_t \rho + \nabla\!\cdot\!(\rho\,\mathbf{u}) = 0, \qquad \mathbf{u} = \frac{\nabla S}{m}$$

**Hamilton–Jacobi equation:**

$$\partial_t S + \frac{|\nabla S|^2}{2m} + V + Q = 0, \qquad Q = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\nabla^2\sqrt{\rho}}{\sqrt{\rho}}$$

where $Q$ is the quantum potential.

The Madelung system has two dynamical objects: the density field $\rho(\mathbf{x},t)$ and the phase field $S(\mathbf{x},t)$, or equivalently the velocity field $\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x},t) = \nabla S/m$.

### 3.2 What ED Has vs. What Madelung Has

| Madelung | ED |
|----------|-----|
| $\rho(\mathbf{x},t)$ — density field | $\rho(\mathbf{x},t)$ — density field |
| $\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x},t) = \nabla S/m$ — velocity field | **absent** |
| $S(\mathbf{x},t)$ — phase field | **absent** |
| — | $v(t)$ — global participation scalar |

ED has *fewer* degrees of freedom than Madelung. It replaces the spatially-resolved velocity field $\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x},t)$ with a single global scalar $v(t)$. This is the critical structural difference.

### 3.3 The Natural Identification

The most parsimonious identification consistent with the three constraints (Section 2.4) is:

$$\boxed{v(t) \;\longleftrightarrow\; \langle \mathbf{u} \rangle = \frac{1}{\mathrm{Vol}}\int \frac{\nabla S}{m}\,d^d x}$$

**$v(t)$ is ED's representation of the spatially-averaged Madelung velocity.**

### 3.4 The $v(t)$ Equation as Averaged Euler Equation

In the Madelung picture, the velocity field satisfies the Euler equation:

$$\partial_t \mathbf{u} + (\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u} = -\frac{1}{m}\nabla(V + Q)$$

Averaging over the spatial domain:

$$\frac{d\langle \mathbf{u}\rangle}{dt} = -\frac{1}{m}\langle\nabla(V+Q)\rangle - \langle(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}\rangle + \text{boundary terms}$$

The convective term $\langle(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}\rangle$ creates a closure problem: the average of the product is not the product of the averages. Information about the spatial structure of $\mathbf{u}$ is needed to close the equation.

**ED's $v(t)$ equation is a specific closure of this hierarchy.** It replaces the full spatial dynamics with:

$$\dot{v} = -\gamma v + H\bar{F}[\rho]$$

where:

- The $H\bar{F}[\rho]$ term captures the mean force $-\langle\nabla(V+Q)\rangle/m$
- The $-\gamma v$ term absorbs the information lost by compressing $\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x},t) \to v(t)$

### 3.5 Physical Content of the Damping $\gamma$

The damping rate $\gamma$ represents the decorrelation between the true velocity field and its spatial mean — the information that is lost in the mean-field compression.

- In closed systems with coherent states: $\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x},t) \approx \mathbf{u}_0(t)$ is nearly uniform, the compression is nearly exact, and $\gamma$ is small.
- In open systems or decoherent states: $\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x},t)$ has rich spatial structure (interference, vortices), the compression loses significant information, and $\gamma$ is large.

$\gamma$ is therefore interpretable as a **decoherence rate** in the quantum regime — the rate at which spatial velocity correlations are lost.

### 3.6 Where the Identification Holds and Breaks

The identification $v(t) \leftrightarrow \langle\mathbf{u}\rangle$ is exact in two limits:

1. **Coherent states** ($\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x},t) \approx \mathbf{u}_0(t)$, nearly uniform velocity): $v(t) \approx u_0(t)$ is an excellent approximation.

2. **Spatially uniform $\rho$** (the RC/Debye limit): there is no spatial structure to average over, and the telegraph equation is exact.

It breaks when $\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x},t)$ has rich spatial structure — interference patterns, vortices, multi-valued phase. In those cases, $\langle\mathbf{u}\rangle$ is a poor summary of the velocity field, and ED's $v(t)$ will diverge from the full Madelung description.

**This divergence is itself a prediction.** ED asserts that the mean-field closure is the correct physics — that the global flow captures the essential dynamics — not just a convenient approximation. Any experiment comparing ED predictions to full Schrödinger evolution in the high-interference regime would test this assertion.

### 3.7 Connection to Nelson's Stochastic Mechanics

In Nelson's stochastic-mechanics framework, the Madelung picture acquires a diffusion interpretation. The particle undergoes Brownian motion with diffusion coefficient $D = \hbar/(2m)$, and two velocity fields emerge:

- **Current velocity:** $\mathbf{v}_c = \nabla S/m$ (the Madelung velocity, directed drift)
- **Osmotic velocity:** $\mathbf{b} = \frac{\hbar}{2m}\frac{\nabla\rho}{\rho} = D\,\nabla(\ln\rho)$ (the quantum diffusion counterpart)

The forward and backward velocities are $\mathbf{v}_\pm = \mathbf{v}_c \pm \mathbf{b}$.

The spatial average of the osmotic velocity $\langle\mathbf{b}\rangle$ depends on boundary conditions and the shape of $\rho$. For symmetric distributions, $\langle\mathbf{b}\rangle = 0$ by parity. Therefore:

$$v(t) \;\longleftrightarrow\; \langle \mathbf{v}_c \rangle = \langle \nabla S/m \rangle$$

The participation field is the mean *current* velocity — the coherent drift — not the osmotic part. This is physically sensible: the osmotic velocity is the "quantum diffusion" that maintains $\rho$'s shape; the current velocity is the actual directed flow. ED's $v(t)$ captures the flow and is insensitive to the osmotic fluctuations.

### 3.8 Does $v(t)$ Map to a Known Quantum Quantity?

**Partially yes, partially no.**

| Aspect | Maps to known quantity? | Details |
|--------|------------------------|---------|
| $v(t)$ as spatial average of $\mathbf{u}$ | **Yes** | Ehrenfest mean velocity $\langle\mathbf{p}\rangle/m$ |
| Damping $-\gamma v$ | **Yes, in open QM** | Lindblad dissipation; no counterpart in closed-system QM |
| Coupling $H$ (feedback strength) | **No** | Standard QM has no parameter controlling mean-field feedback on $\rho$ |
| Telegraph structure | **No** | Standard QM does not produce telegraph dynamics in $\rho$ |

**Conclusion:** $v(t)$ is the Ehrenfest mean velocity *generalised* with dissipation ($\gamma$) and controllable mean-field coupling ($H$). It contains standard QM as the limit $\gamma \to 0$, $H \to 0$, and goes beyond it when either is nonzero.

## 4. The Compton Frequency Anchoring

### 4.1 The Propagation Speed

The telegraph equation, in its spatially-extended form, propagates information at finite speed. For the ED coupled system in the long-wavelength limit, the characteristic propagation speed is:

$$c_{\text{ED}}^2 \sim D \cdot H$$

The precise proportionality constant depends on the penalty and mobility parameters, but the dimensional scaling is set by $DH$.

### 4.2 The Causality Requirement

In the quantum regime, $D = \hbar/(2m)$. The question is: **what value of $H$ makes the propagation speed equal to the speed of light?**

$$c^2 = \frac{\hbar}{2m} \cdot H \quad \Longrightarrow \quad H = \frac{2mc^2}{\hbar} \equiv 2\omega_C$$

where $\omega_C = mc^2/\hbar$ is the Compton angular frequency.

$$\boxed{H = \frac{2mc^2}{\hbar} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad c_{\text{ED}} = c}$$

**If the participation coupling in the quantum regime equals twice the Compton frequency, then the ED telegraph equation propagates at the speed of light.**

### 4.3 Numerical Verification

| Particle | $D = \hbar/(2m)$ | $H = 2mc^2/\hbar$ | $c_{\text{ED}} = \sqrt{DH}$ |
|----------|---|---|---|
| Electron | $5.79 \times 10^{-5}$ m²/s | $1.55 \times 10^{21}$ s⁻¹ | $3.00 \times 10^{8}$ m/s |
| Proton | $3.15 \times 10^{-8}$ m²/s | $2.85 \times 10^{24}$ s⁻¹ | $3.00 \times 10^{8}$ m/s |
| Planck mass | $2.42 \times 10^{-43}$ m²/s | $3.71 \times 10^{59}$ s⁻¹ | $3.00 \times 10^{8}$ m/s |

The result is exact and mass-independent: $c_{\text{ED}} = \sqrt{(\hbar/2m)(2mc^2/\hbar)} = c$ for all $m$.

### 4.4 Why This Anchoring Is Not Optional

The standard ED PDE without participation ($H = 0$) is parabolic. Parabolic equations propagate at infinite speed — a delta-function initial condition produces nonzero density everywhere at any $t > 0$. This is the same mathematical pathology as the Schrödinger equation's "instantaneous spreading."

Setting $H = 2\omega_C$ converts the short-time behaviour from parabolic (infinite speed) to hyperbolic (speed $c$). The system transitions from wave-like at times $t \lesssim 1/\gamma$ to diffusion-like at times $t \gg 1/\gamma$.

**The participation channel is not a speculative addition to the ED PDE. It is the mechanism by which ED respects the speed of light in the quantum regime.**

### 4.5 The Wave–Diffusion Interpolation

The ratio $\gamma/H$ controls the character of propagation:

| Regime | Condition | Behaviour |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Ultrarelativistic (wave-like) | $\gamma \ll H$ | Telegraph → wave equation, propagation at speed $c$ |
| Intermediate | $\gamma \sim H$ | Damped oscillatory propagation |
| Nonrelativistic (diffusion-like) | $\gamma \gg H$ | Telegraph → diffusion equation, infinite effective speed |

The transition from wave to diffusion physics is controlled by $\gamma/H$. For $H = 2\omega_C$, the Compton timescale $\tau_C = 1/\omega_C \sim 10^{-21}$ s (electron) sets the crossover: at times shorter than $\tau_C$, propagation is wave-like; at times longer, it becomes diffusion-like.

### 4.6 Connection to Zitterbewegung

The oscillation frequency in the uniform limit is:

$$\omega_0^2 = D^2 P_0^2 + H^2 D P_0$$

With $H = 2\omega_C$ dominating (i.e., $H^2 D P_0 \gg D^2 P_0^2$):

$$\omega_0 \approx H\sqrt{D P_0} = 2\omega_C \sqrt{D P_0}$$

This rapid oscillation at a frequency proportional to the Compton frequency is structurally reminiscent of Zitterbewegung — the rapid trembling motion predicted by the Dirac equation. In ED, it arises naturally from the participation channel, not from spin or antiparticle interference.

### 4.7 Falsifiability

The Compton anchoring makes a specific prediction: **quantum probability density propagates at finite speed $c$, with a sharp wavefront.**

- Standard Schrödinger equation: infinite propagation speed (exponentially small but nonzero superluminal tails).
- ED with $H = 2\omega_C$: strict propagation speed $c$ (no superluminal tails).

This is testable in principle via tunnelling-time experiments, arrival-time distributions, or any setup measuring the leading edge of a probability wavefront.

## 6. Numerical Experiments to Isolate $v(t)$

All experiments use the ED-SIM-02 simulation engine and require no code modifications — only parameter choices.

### 6.1 Experiment 1: $H$-Transition in the PME Regime

**Purpose:** Identify the participation threshold — the critical $H$ at which $v(t)$ begins to dominate the dynamics.

**Setup:** Barenblatt initial condition (compact bump), $\beta = 2$, $P_0 = 0.01$, $D = 0.3$, $d = 2$.

**Protocol:** Run the identical initial condition at $H = 0, 0.1, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 50, 100, 200$.

**Observables:**

| Observable | Expected behaviour |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Front position $R(t)$ | Unchanged at small $H$; oscillatory modulation at large $H$ |
| $v(t)$ time series | Zero at $H=0$; oscillatory onset at some $H_c$ |
| $\langle\rho\rangle(t)$ | Monotonic at $H=0$; damped oscillation at large $H$ |
| Energy $E(t)$ | Monotonically decreasing (Law 2) — test whether this survives at all $H$ |

**Key question:** Is there a sharp $H_c$ below which $v(t)$ is negligible and above which it dominates? If so, $H_c$ defines a "participation threshold" — a phase boundary in the PDE's parameter space.

### 6.2 Experiment 2: Coherence Test (Single Peak vs. Two Peaks)

**Purpose:** Test the $v(t) \leftrightarrow \langle\mathbf{u}\rangle$ identification directly.

**Setup:** Two initial conditions at identical $(D, M_0, \beta, P_0, H)$:

- **IC-A:** Single Gaussian peak (nearly coherent, $\langle\mathbf{u}\rangle$ well-defined, net drift expected)
- **IC-B:** Two symmetric Gaussian peaks (rich spatial structure, $\langle\mathbf{u}\rangle \approx 0$ by symmetry)

**Protocol:** Evolve both and compare $v(t)$ amplitudes.

**Prediction:** If $v(t) \leftrightarrow \langle\mathbf{u}\rangle$, then IC-A should show large $v(t)$ oscillations while IC-B should show suppressed $v(t)$. The ratio $|v_A|/|v_B|$ quantifies how well the mean-velocity interpretation works.

### 6.3 Experiment 3: Propagation Speed Measurement

**Purpose:** Measure the finite propagation speed introduced by the participation channel.

**Setup:** Narrow density pulse at $x = 0$, large domain ($L \gg$ pulse width), $H > 0$. Track the arrival time of the density perturbation at positions $x = L/4, L/2, 3L/4$.

**Protocol:** At $H = 0$, the pulse spreads diffusively (arrival time $\sim L^2/D$). At $H > 0$, the telegraph structure should give a finite propagation speed, so arrival time should scale as $L/c_{\text{ED}}$ for large $L$.

**Observables:**

| Measurement | $H = 0$ (diffusion) | $H > 0$ (telegraph) |
|------------|-------|---------|
| Arrival time $t_{\text{arr}}(L)$ | $\propto L^2$ | $\propto L$ |
| Leading-edge profile | Gaussian tail (infinite speed) | Sharp front (finite speed) |
| Extracted speed | $\infty$ (no well-defined speed) | $c_{\text{ED}} = \sqrt{DH}$ |

**What this tests:** The most fundamental prediction of the participation channel — finite-speed propagation. If confirmed numerically, this is the single strongest argument for the telegraph interpretation.

**What this tests:** The temporal tension mechanism in full generality. If $v(t)$ creates an effective inter-peak potential with a well, that predicts bound-state formation mediated purely by the participation channel — a phenomenon with no counterpart in standard diffusion theory.

## 7. Predictions Beyond Standard Physics

### 7.1 Prediction 1: Finite-Speed Quantum Probability Propagation

**What ED predicts:** Probability density propagates at speed $c$ when $H = 2\omega_C$. The leading edge of a propagating probability distribution has a sharp cutoff — no superluminal tails.

**What standard QM predicts:** The Schrödinger equation is parabolic: probability density propagates at infinite speed. A Gaussian wave packet produces nonzero probability everywhere at any $t > 0$, though the superluminal amplitude is exponentially small.

**How to test:** Tunnelling-time experiments (Larmor clock, attoclock), arrival-time distributions, or time-of-flight measurements sensitive to the leading edge of the probability wavefront.

**Falsification criterion:** If quantum probability density propagates faster than $c$ in any experiment, the Compton anchoring is wrong. If there is a sharp cutoff at $c$ (as ED predicts), the anchoring is confirmed.

### 7.2 Prediction 2: Telegraph Oscillations in Dense Colloids

**What ED predicts:** In concentrated colloidal suspensions ($\phi > 0.3$), a concentration perturbation should relax with damped oscillations, not monotonic exponential decay. The oscillation frequency scales as $\omega \sim \sqrt{DH}$ where $D$ is the collective diffusion coefficient and $H$ is the hydrodynamic coupling strength.

**What standard diffusion predicts:** Fick's second law gives purely monotonic relaxation: $\delta(t) = \delta_0 e^{-t/\tau}$ with no oscillation at any concentration.

**How to test:** Concentration-step experiment in dense colloidal suspension. Track concentration profile with confocal microscopy at sub-millisecond resolution. Look for overshoot and ringing in the approach to equilibrium.

**Falsification criterion:** If relaxation is purely monotonic at all concentrations and measurement resolutions, the participation channel is absent in condensed matter.

### 7.3 Prediction 3: Activity-Dependent Rotation Curves

**What ED predicts:** Because $v(t)$ equilibrates on a finite timescale $\tau_{\text{eq}} \sim 1/\gamma$, galaxies with recent activity (star formation, accretion) should have $v(t)$ out of equilibrium and therefore show systematic deviations from the equilibrium BTFR prediction. Specifically, star-forming galaxies should scatter *above* the BTFR at fixed baryonic mass.

**What $\Lambda$CDM predicts:** Dark matter halos do not couple to baryonic activity on short timescales. No systematic difference between active and quiescent galaxies at fixed halo mass.

**What MOND predicts:** Modified gravity is instantaneous (algebraic relation between acceleration and baryonic mass). No activity dependence.

**How to test:** Compare rotation curves of star-forming vs. quiescent galaxies at fixed baryonic mass in SPARC or MaNGA survey data.

**Falsification criterion:** If no systematic activity dependence exists at $>2\sigma$ significance, the temporal tension mechanism is wrong.

### 7.4 Prediction 4: Merger Lag

**What ED predicts:** Recently merged galaxy pairs should show transient BTFR violations because their combined $v(t)$ has not yet equilibrated to the new combined mass. The relaxation timescale is $\tau_{\text{eq}} \sim 1/\gamma$, expected to be several hundred Myr for typical galaxy parameters. The deviation should correlate with time-since-merger and decay exponentially.

**What $\Lambda$CDM predicts:** Post-merger relaxation occurs via dynamical friction and violent relaxation of the dark matter halo, on timescales of $\sim 1$ Gyr. The BTFR deviation is controlled by halo properties, not baryonic dynamics.

**What MOND predicts:** No transient deviation (instantaneous response).

**How to test:** Identify post-merger galaxies (morphological signatures, tidal features) and measure BTFR residuals. Correlate residuals with estimated time-since-merger from tidal-feature age.

**Falsification criterion:** If post-merger BTFR residuals are uncorrelated with merger age, the $v(t)$ equilibration mechanism is wrong.

### 7.5 Prediction 5: Oscillatory Deceleration Parameter

The deceleration parameter $q(z)$ should have subtle oscillatory deviations with a characteristic frequency set by $\sqrt{D \cdot H}$ in cosmological units.

## 9. Open Questions

1. **Physical origin of $\gamma$ in each regime.** The damping rate $\gamma$ is the least constrained parameter. In the quantum regime, it maps to decoherence; in condensed matter, to viscous drag; in galaxies, to dynamical friction. But can $\gamma$ be derived from the other ED parameters $(D, M_0, \beta, P_0)$, or is it genuinely independent? If independent, it is ED's only free parameter not fixed by the architecture.

2. **Multi-particle generalisation.** The current $v(t)$ is a single global scalar. For systems with multiple species or multiple length scales, does ED require multiple participation fields $v_1(t), v_2(t), \ldots$? If so, how do they couple?

3. **Connection to Berry phase.** In the quantum regime, the phase $S(\mathbf{x},t)$ contains geometric (Berry) phase contributions when the Hamiltonian is cycled adiabatically. Does $v(t) = \langle\nabla S/m\rangle$ capture Berry-phase effects, or does the spatial averaging erase them?

4. **Does the Compton anchoring extend to other regimes?** The causality requirement $c_{\text{ED}} = c$ uniquely fixes $H$ in any regime where $D$ is known. In the galactic regime ($D = v_{\text{circ}} \cdot L_0$), this would give $H = c^2/(v_{\text{circ}} \cdot L_0)$. Is this physically reasonable? Does it match the $H$ values that produce the correct BTFR?

5. **Can $\gamma$ be measured independently?** If $\gamma$ is the decoherence rate (quantum), or the viscous damping rate (condensed matter), or the dynamical friction timescale (galactic), then it should be measurable independently of ED. Comparing the independently-measured $\gamma$ with the value needed to fit ED predictions would be a strong consistency check.

6. **The $\gamma \to 0$ limit.** When $\gamma = 0$ (no damping), the telegraph equation becomes the wave equation. Does ED in the $\gamma = 0$ limit correspond to a known physical theory? In the quantum regime with $H = 2\omega_C$ and $\gamma = 0$, is the result equivalent to the Klein–Gordon equation or some other relativistic wave equation?

7. **Spatial generalisation of $v(t)$.** Could $v(t)$ be promoted to a field $v(\mathbf{x},t)$ in a future version of ED? If so, the ED PDE would become a system of two coupled PDEs — structurally similar to Madelung itself. Would this recover the full Schrödinger equation as a limit?

## Appendix A: Derivation of the Spatially-Averaged Euler Equation

Starting from the Madelung–Euler equation:

$$\partial_t \mathbf{u} + (\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u} = -\frac{1}{m}\nabla(V + Q)$$

Integrate over the spatial domain $\Omega$ with volume $|\Omega|$:

$$\frac{d}{dt}\langle\mathbf{u}\rangle = -\frac{1}{m}\langle\nabla(V + Q)\rangle - \langle(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}\rangle$$

The convective term can be decomposed as:

$$\langle(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}\rangle = \langle\mathbf{u}\rangle \cdot \langle\nabla\mathbf{u}\rangle + \langle\mathbf{u}'\cdot\nabla\mathbf{u}'\rangle$$

where $\mathbf{u}' = \mathbf{u} - \langle\mathbf{u}\rangle$ is the fluctuation. The second term is the Reynolds-stress-like closure term.

**ED's closure:** Replace $\langle\mathbf{u}'\cdot\nabla\mathbf{u}'\rangle$ with $\gamma\langle\mathbf{u}\rangle$, a linear damping proportional to the mean velocity. This is the simplest closure that:

- Is dissipative (removes energy from the mean flow)
- Vanishes when $\mathbf{u}$ is spatially uniform ($\mathbf{u}' = 0$)
- Has a single free parameter ($\gamma$)

The result is:

$$\frac{d\langle\mathbf{u}\rangle}{dt} = -\gamma\langle\mathbf{u}\rangle - \frac{1}{m}\langle\nabla(V + Q)\rangle - \langle\mathbf{u}\rangle \cdot \langle\nabla\mathbf{u}\rangle$$

Identifying $v(t) = \langle\mathbf{u}\rangle$ and absorbing the last term into $\gamma$ (or dropping it for nearly uniform flow):

$$\dot{v} = -\gamma v + H\bar{F}[\rho]$$

where $H\bar{F}[\rho]$ captures $-\langle\nabla(V+Q)\rangle/m$ through the ED constitutive channels.

## Appendix B: Telegraph Equation Propagation Speed

### B.1 The Spatially-Extended Telegraph Equation

Consider the ED coupled system linearised around the equilibrium $\rho = \rho^*$, $v = 0$, with small perturbations $\delta(\mathbf{x},t) = \rho - \rho^*$ and $v(t)$:

$$\partial_t \delta = D\nabla^2\delta - D P_0 \delta + Hv$$
$$\dot{v} = -\gamma v + H(D\langle\nabla^2\delta\rangle - D P_0\langle\delta\rangle)$$

For a single Fourier mode $\delta \propto e^{ikx}$, the spatial average $\langle\nabla^2\delta\rangle = -k^2\langle\delta\rangle$ and $\langle\delta\rangle = \delta$ (for a single mode).

Eliminating $v$:

$$\ddot{\delta} + (Dk^2 + DP_0 + \gamma)\dot{\delta} + \gamma(Dk^2 + DP_0)\delta + H^2 DP_0\delta + H^2 Dk^2\delta = 0$$

In the long-wavelength limit ($k \to 0$), the $k^2$ terms are subdominant. In the short-wavelength limit ($k$ large), the leading terms give:

$$\ddot{\delta} + Dk^2\dot{\delta} \approx -H^2 Dk^2 \delta$$

The dispersion relation gives a propagation speed:

$$c_{\text{ED}}^2 = \lim_{k\to\infty} \frac{\omega^2}{k^2} = D \cdot H$$

### B.2 The Compton Result

Substituting $D = \hbar/(2m)$ and $H = 2mc^2/\hbar$:

$$c_{\text{ED}}^2 = \frac{\hbar}{2m} \cdot \frac{2mc^2}{\hbar} = c^2$$

$$\boxed{c_{\text{ED}} = c}$$

This result is exact, independent of $P_0$, $\gamma$, $M_0$, or $\beta$.
