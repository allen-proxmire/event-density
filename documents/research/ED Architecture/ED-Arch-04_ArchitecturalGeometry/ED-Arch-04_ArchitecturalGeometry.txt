1. PURPOSE OF ARCHITECTURAL GEOMETRY
========================================================================

Geometry, in the ED context, is the study of the shapes that the
architecture creates in its state spaces. Where laws state what must
hold and motifs describe what recurs, geometry describes where things
happen: the surfaces, curves, flows, and boundaries that organize the
PDE's dynamics in space.

Architectural geometry operates at three levels:

  (i)   PHYSICAL SPACE GEOMETRY.  The spatial shapes of density
        profiles: peaks, troughs, gradients, curvatures. These are
        the shapes that exist at any instant in the simulation domain.

  (ii)  PHASE SPACE GEOMETRY.  The shapes of trajectories in the
        reduced coordinate system (rho_peak, v_peak): spirals, curves,
        straight lines, fixed points. These are the shapes that the
        system traces as it evolves.

  (iii) PARAMETER SPACE GEOMETRY.  The shapes of regions in the
        (D, zeta, tau) parameter space: boundaries between flow types,
        surfaces of constant behavior, gradients of dynamical
        properties. These are the shapes that organize the
        architecture's operating range.

Geometry differs from motifs and laws in the following way:

  - LAWS are constraints (what must be true).
  - MOTIFS are patterns (what recurs).
  - GEOMETRY is structure (where things are located and how they
    are shaped).

Laws constrain geometry: the Law of Unique Attraction requires that
phase space have exactly one sink. Motifs inhabit geometry: the Spiral
motif traces a curve on the Spiral Sheet. Geometry organizes both:
it is the spatial framework within which laws operate and motifs live.

The progression of the ED-Arch series is therefore:

  ED-Arch-01: What the architecture does (summary).
  ED-Arch-02: What patterns recur (motifs).
  ED-Arch-03: What rules govern (laws).
  ED-Arch-04: What shapes result (geometry).

Geometry completes the structural description by making the abstract
laws and motifs spatially concrete. A law says the manifold is one-
dimensional; geometry says it is a curve with specific curvature that
spirals in one regime and straightens in another. A motif says the
Boundary exists; geometry says it is a plane in parameter space with
equation D + 2*zeta = 1.

2. THE GEOMETRIC OBJECTS OF ED

------------------------------------------------------------------------
(a) THE ATTRACTOR POINT
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Name:       The Attractor Point
Definition: The unique fixed point of the unified PDE in the reduced
            phase space, located at (rho_peak, v_peak) = (rho*, 0),
            where rho* = 50 is the penalty equilibrium.

Governing laws:
  - Law of Unique Attraction (guarantees existence and uniqueness).
  - Law of Transient Structure (guarantees that all trajectories
    reach it).

Associated motifs:
  - All motifs terminate at the Attractor Point. It is the endpoint
    of every Spiral, the target of every Dissipator trajectory, and
    the center of every Manifold.

Geometric description:
  In the reduced (rho_peak, v_peak) phase plane, the Attractor Point
  is a dimensionless object: a single point. Its local geometry is
  determined by the linearized dynamics around it.

  Below the Critical Damping Boundary (D + 2*zeta < 1):
    The Attractor Point is a STABLE FOCUS. The linearized eigenvalues
    are complex with negative real part. Trajectories spiral inward.
    The local geometry is a logarithmic spiral with pitch determined
    by the ratio of the eigenvalues' imaginary to real parts. At
    (D=0, zeta=0.25), the spiral is tight (curvature 505,631 in
    ED-Phys-32). At (D=0.5, zeta=0.25), the spiral is looser
    (curvature 160,390).

  Above the Critical Damping Boundary (D + 2*zeta > 1):
    The Attractor Point is a STABLE NODE. The linearized eigenvalues
    are real and negative. Trajectories approach along straight or
    nearly straight paths. The local geometry is a star node (if
    eigenvalues are equal) or a degenerate node (if unequal). At
    (D=1.0, zeta=1.0), curvature collapses to 10.6 (ED-Phys-32),
    confirming near-linear approach.

  At the Critical Damping Boundary (D + 2*zeta = 1):
    The Attractor Point transitions between focus and node. The
    eigenvalues are real and coincident, creating a degenerate
    node. This is the geometric signature of critical damping.

Where it appears in ED-Phys:
  - ED-Phys-29: all 66 trajectories converge to rho_peak = 50.0,
    v_peak = 0.0 within machine precision (final_std ~ 10^-14).
  - ED-Phys-30: all 48 probes converge to the same point.
  - ED-Phys-33: global attractor confirmed at (50.0, 0.0) across
    all 135 atlas runs. Basin of attraction: entire physical
    state space.

(b) THE STABLE MANIFOLD CURVE
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Name:       The Stable Manifold Curve
Definition: The one-dimensional curve in the (rho_peak, v_peak)
            phase plane onto which all trajectories collapse after
            the initial transient, and along which they converge
            to the Attractor Point.

Governing laws:
  - Law of One-Dimensional Relaxation (guarantees the curve exists).
  - Law of Modal Hierarchy (explains why it is one-dimensional).

Associated motifs:
  - The Manifold motif is this curve.
  - The Spiral motif is the winding motion along and around this curve.
  - The Dissipator motif is the straight sliding along this curve.

Geometric description:
  The Stable Manifold Curve is not a straight line. Its shape depends
  on the parameter regime:

  OSCILLATORY REGIME (D = 0, H = 1):
    The curve is a tightly wound spiral in the (rho_peak, v_peak)
    plane. Its v-spread (the range of v values traversed during
    convergence) is 0.178 (ED-Phys-30). The spiral winds inward
    with curvature up to 69,277 (ED-Phys-30, mix_A40B1.0_k8 probe).
    The curve makes many revolutions around the Attractor Point
    before reaching it: 112 to 235 crossings in ED-Phys-29
    oscillatory runs.

  HYBRID REGIME (D = 0.5, H = 0.5):
    The curve is a loosely wound spiral that transitions to near-
    straight as it approaches the Attractor Point. Its v-spread is
    0.168 (ED-Phys-30), only slightly narrower than oscillatory.
    Mean curvature drops to near zero in many runs, indicating that
    the spiral is open enough to be locally approximated by a curve
    of modest bending. Oscillatory approach in 13/16 probes (81%),
    monotonic in 3/16 (19%).

  PARABOLIC REGIME (D = 1, H = 0):
    The curve is nearly straight. Its v-spread collapses to 0.047
    (ED-Phys-30), a 3.8x compression relative to oscillatory.
    Curvature is zero in all 16 probes. The manifold geometry is
    effectively a radial ray pointing from the initial condition
    toward the Attractor Point, traversed monotonically.

  The manifold's width (v-spread) is therefore a geometric invariant
  that characterizes the regime: wide and wound in oscillatory, narrow
  and straight in parabolic.

Where it appears in ED-Phys:
  - ED-Phys-29: manifold dimension = 1 in 64/66 trajectories (97%).
  - ED-Phys-30: manifold dimension = 1 in 48/48 trajectories (100%).
    Approach direction: density-dominated in 13/16 at D=0.5 and
    D=1.0; mixed in oscillatory.
  - ED-Phys-32: the manifold curve is the backbone of every phase
    portrait. All 25 trajectories per portrait converge onto it.

(c) THE SPIRAL SHEET
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Name:       The Spiral Sheet
Definition: The two-dimensional surface in the extended state space
            (rho_peak, v_peak, t) swept out by the family of all
            spiral trajectories that exist below the Critical Damping
            Boundary. Equivalently, in the parameter-augmented phase
            space (rho_peak, v_peak, D, zeta), it is the union of
            all spiral manifold curves for D + 2*zeta < 1.

Governing laws:
  - Law of Critical Damping Boundary (defines the sheet's domain of
    existence).
  - Law of One-Dimensional Relaxation (each trajectory on the sheet
    is one-dimensional).

Associated motifs:
  - Spiral (the defining motif of this surface).
  - Memory Loop (the dynamical mechanism that creates the winding).

Geometric description:
  The Spiral Sheet is a ruled surface: it is swept out by a family
  of one-dimensional curves (the individual spiral trajectories),
  parameterized by the initial condition. Each curve spirals inward
  toward the Attractor Point, and the entire family of curves fills
  a two-dimensional region of phase space.

  The sheet's geometry varies across its domain:

  NEAR D = 0, ZETA = 0.25 (deep oscillatory):
    The sheet is tightly wound with many layers. Each spiral
    trajectory makes dozens to hundreds of revolutions. The layers
    are closely spaced, reflecting the small amplitude decay per
    cycle. Phase-space curvature reaches 505,631 (ED-Phys-32).
    The sheet resembles a compressed spring viewed end-on.

  NEAR D = 0.5, ZETA = 0.25 (boundary regime):
    The sheet is loosely wound with 6-7 revolutions per trajectory
    (ED-Phys-34). The layers are widely spaced. The sheet is
    beginning to flatten as it approaches the Critical Damping
    Boundary. Curvature: 160,390 (ED-Phys-32).

  NEAR D + 2*ZETA = 1 (approaching the boundary):
    The sheet degenerates. The spiral makes at most 1-2 revolutions
    before the trajectory straightens. The winding weakens, the
    layers merge, and the sheet collapses toward the Manifold Curve.
    Curvature drops to 209 at (D=0.5, zeta=0.5) (ED-Phys-32).

  AT D + 2*ZETA = 1 (on the boundary):
    The Spiral Sheet ceases to exist. The winding number drops to
    zero, and all trajectories become monotonic. The sheet's edge
    is the Boundary Surface.

  The Spiral Sheet's thickness (measured perpendicular to the Manifold
  Curve) is set by the oscillation amplitude: large initial amplitudes
  create thick sheets, while small amplitudes create thin ones. But
  the sheet always collapses to the Manifold Curve at late times,
  because the Law of Transient Structure requires all structure to
  decay.

Where it appears in ED-Phys:
  - ED-Phys-32: the 15 phase portraits collectively map the Spiral
    Sheet across parameter space. Spiral topology in 6/15 portraits,
    all below the boundary.
  - ED-Phys-29: 22/22 oscillatory trajectories and 19/22 hybrid
    trajectories lie on the Spiral Sheet.
  - ED-Phys-30: spiral approach confirmed in 16/16 oscillatory and
    13/16 hybrid probes.

(d) THE MONOTONIC CONE
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Name:       The Monotonic Cone
Definition: The region of the parameter-augmented phase space
            (rho_peak, v_peak, D, zeta) in which all trajectories
            approach the Attractor Point without oscillation. It
            occupies the parameter domain D + 2*zeta > 1 and all
            of phase space within that domain.

Governing laws:
  - Law of Critical Damping Boundary (defines the cone's domain).
  - Law of One-Dimensional Relaxation (trajectories inside the cone
    are one-dimensional).
  - Law of Transient Structure (trajectories traverse the cone in
    finite time).

Associated motifs:
  - Dissipator (the dominant motif inside the cone).
  - Manifold (the cone's central axis).

Geometric description:
  The Monotonic Cone is named for its approximate shape in phase
  space. At any fixed parameter point above the boundary, the set
  of all trajectories forms a cone-like region centered on the
  Attractor Point, with its apex at the attractor and its opening
  facing outward toward large deviations from equilibrium.

  The cone is not circular. It is elongated along the rho_peak
  axis and compressed along the v_peak axis, because:
  (1) Density perturbations persist longer than velocity perturbations
      (the penalty acts on rho; damping acts on v directly).
  (2) At D = 1.0, the v-spread is only 0.047, creating a cone
      that is almost a line (the Manifold Curve itself).

  The cone's opening angle decreases as damping increases:
  - At (D=0.5, zeta=0.5), just above the boundary, the cone is
    relatively wide. Curvature is 209, indicating slight bending
    of trajectories.
  - At (D=1.0, zeta=1.0), deep in the monotonic regime, the cone
    is extremely narrow. Curvature is 10.6, indicating near-straight
    trajectories.
  - At (D=1.0, zeta=0.25), curvature is 72.0, slightly wider due
    to lower damping but still monotonic.

  The cone's boundary (where it meets the Spiral Sheet at the
  Critical Damping Boundary) is not a sharp geometric edge in
  phase space. Individual trajectories near the boundary may show
  weak damped transients that are neither clearly spiral nor clearly
  monotonic. This is the geometric origin of the 4 classifier
  failures in ED-Phys-34.

Where it appears in ED-Phys:
  - ED-Phys-29: 20/22 parabolic trajectories are purely monotonic
    (2 show transient damped oscillation).
  - ED-Phys-30: 16/16 parabolic probes are monotonic (100%).
  - ED-Phys-31: D=1.0 gives 0 cycles across all 27 runs.
  - ED-Phys-32: monotonic topology in 9/15 phase portraits, all
    above the Critical Damping Boundary. Mean curvature range:
    10.6 to 209.

(e) THE HORIZON SURFACE
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Name:       The Horizon Surface
Definition: The hypersurface in state space at which the mobility
            function M(rho) has collapsed to a small fraction of its
            maximum value, effectively halting diffusive transport.
            Geometrically, it is the set of states where rho
            approaches rho_max at any spatial point.

Governing laws:
  - Law of Finite Capacity (the mobility vanishes at rho_max,
    creating this surface).
  - Law of Transient Structure (trajectories that reach the surface
    are always reflected back by the penalty).

Associated motifs:
  - Horizon (the motif defined by this surface).
  - Spiral (the motif that can drive trajectories into the surface
    repeatedly at D = 0).

Geometric description:
  In the full infinite-dimensional state space, the Horizon Surface
  is the set of all density profiles rho(x) such that max_x(rho(x))
  = rho_threshold, where the effective threshold is approximately
  0.9 * rho_max = 90 (the point at which M has fallen to about 1%
  of its peak value).

  In the reduced phase space (rho_peak, v_peak), the Horizon Surface
  projects to a vertical line at rho_peak = rho_threshold. This line
  is the boundary of the kinetically accessible region: trajectories
  can approach it from the left but cannot cross it.

  The surface's geometry has three key features:

  REFLECTIVITY:
    Trajectories that reach the Horizon Surface are reflected. The
    penalty P_SY2 continues to operate at the surface (it depends
    on rho, not on nabla(rho)), and it always points inward. The
    surface acts as a reflective barrier, not an absorbing one.

  TRANSIENCE:
    No trajectory remains on the surface. The penalty's restoring
    force pulls rho back toward rho* = 50, far from the horizon at
    rho ~ 90. The residence time on the surface is brief: typically
    a fraction of one oscillation cycle.

  CONDITIONAL EXISTENCE:
    The Horizon Surface is only dynamically relevant when trajectories
    can reach it. This requires D = 0 (pure oscillatory, maximizing
    the memory loop's amplitude) and A >= 40 (sufficient initial
    amplitude to drive rho near rho_max). At D >= 0.25, the diffusion
    channel dissipates amplitude before it can reach the surface.

  The surface's interaction with the Spiral Sheet creates a
  characteristic geometric pattern: the spiral trajectory bounces
  off the Horizon Surface and reverses direction, creating a flat
  segment in the rho_peak time series followed by a sharp reversal.
  In ED-Phys-29, horizon_max reached 7 density units above the
  nominal threshold at D=0, A=80, indicating brief penetration
  into the low-mobility zone.

Where it appears in ED-Phys:
  - ED-Phys-29: horizon activated in 8/22 oscillatory runs (36%),
    0/22 hybrid, 0/22 parabolic.
  - ED-Phys-33: horizon fraction 33% at D=0, 0% at D >= 0.25.
  - ED-Phys-34: horizon_max = 1 at D=0, gauss_A40.

(f) THE BOUNDARY SURFACE
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Name:       The Boundary Surface
Definition: The surface in the (D, zeta) parameter plane defined by
            the equation D + 2*zeta = 1, which separates the spiral
            flow region from the monotonic flow region.

Governing laws:
  - Law of Critical Damping Boundary (defines this surface).
  - Law of Channel Complementarity (provides the D + H = 1
    constraint from which the boundary emerges).

Associated motifs:
  - Boundary (the motif that is this surface).
  - Spiral (exists only on one side).
  - Dissipator (dominates on the other side).

Geometric description:
  In the two-dimensional (D, zeta) parameter plane, the Boundary
  Surface is a straight line with equation D + 2*zeta = 1, or
  equivalently zeta = (1 - D)/2. It passes through the points:
    (D=0, zeta=0.5)
    (D=0.5, zeta=0.25)
    (D=1.0, zeta=0)

  The line divides the positive quadrant of the (D, zeta) plane
  into two regions:

  SPIRAL REGION (below the line, D + 2*zeta < 1):
    Contains the parameter points where the Spiral Sheet exists.
    The spiral's intensity increases with distance below the line.
    At (D=0, zeta=0.25), the system is maximally oscillatory (49
    mean cycles). At the boundary, the system has approximately
    1 cycle.

  MONOTONIC REGION (above the line, D + 2*zeta > 1):
    Contains the parameter points where the Monotonic Cone exists.
    The monotonic convergence becomes stronger with distance above
    the line. At (D=1.0, zeta=1.0), the system has the lowest
    curvature (10.6) and the most direct approach.

  If tau is included as a third parameter, the Boundary Surface
  extends into three dimensions as the plane D + 2*zeta = 1 for
  all tau. ED-Phys-31 confirms that tau has minimal effect (< 5%
  variation in convergence rate), so the boundary's location is
  effectively tau-independent. The Boundary Surface is a vertical
  plane in (D, zeta, tau) space.

  The line's geometry has an important property: it is LINEAR.
  The boundary is not a curve, not a fractal, not a critical
  surface with complicated topology. It is a straight line (or
  plane in 3D), reflecting the linear structure of the damping
  terms in the v-equation. Nonlinear effects modify the dynamics
  near the boundary (creating the classifier ambiguity zone) but
  do not bend the boundary itself.

Where it appears in ED-Phys:
  - ED-Phys-32: 6 topological transitions documented, all lying
    on or near D + 2*zeta = 1. No transitions observed away from
    this line.
  - ED-Phys-33: spiral fraction transitions from 56% to 0% as
    D increases from 0 to 1, consistent with the boundary line
    sweeping across the sampled zeta values.
  - ED-Phys-34: the boundary regime at (D=0.5, zeta=0.25) sits
    exactly on the line (0.5 + 2*0.25 = 1.0). It shows 6-7
    cycles, confirming spiral character at the boundary's lower
    edge.

(g) THE MODAL DECAY FUNNEL
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Name:       The Modal Decay Funnel
Definition: The geometric structure in the modal amplitude space
            (a_1, a_2, a_3, ..., a_N) through which trajectories
            pass as higher Fourier modes decay faster than the
            fundamental, funneling the system's state toward the
            one-dimensional manifold dominated by the k=1 mode.

Governing laws:
  - Law of Modal Hierarchy (the k^2 decay rate establishes the
    funnel's shape).
  - Law of One-Dimensional Relaxation (the funnel's output is the
    1D manifold).

Associated motifs:
  - Manifold (the funnel's narrow end).
  - Nonlinear Triad (generates content in the funnel's interior
    through harmonic coupling).

Geometric description:
  Consider the state of the density field decomposed into Fourier
  modes: rho(x,t) = rho* + sum_k a_k(t)*sin(k*pi*x/L). The modal
  amplitudes (a_1, a_2, ..., a_N) define a point in an N-dimensional
  space.

  At t = 0, the initial condition populates many modes. The point
  in modal space can be anywhere. As time evolves, the diffusion
  operator damps each mode at rate proportional to k^2:
    - k=1 decays at rate proportional to 1
    - k=2 decays at rate proportional to 4
    - k=3 decays at rate proportional to 9
    - k=n decays at rate proportional to n^2

  This differential decay creates a funnel: the higher-dimensional
  axes (a_3, a_4, ...) collapse quickly, the intermediate axes (a_2)
  collapse at moderate rate, and the fundamental axis (a_1) persists
  longest.

  The funnel's cross-section at any time t is an ellipsoid whose
  semi-axes shrink as exp(-k^2*lambda*t). The ratio of the k=2
  semi-axis to the k=1 semi-axis shrinks as exp(-3*lambda*t). After
  a few multiples of 1/lambda, the ellipsoid has collapsed to
  essentially one dimension.

  The Nonlinear Triad modifies the funnel's geometry by coupling
  modes. The M'|nabla(rho)|^2 term continuously generates k=3
  content from the k=1 mode. This means the k=3 axis of the funnel
  does not decay to zero independently; instead, it is enslaved to
  the k=1 axis, maintaining a fixed amplitude ratio set by the
  coupling strength. The funnel is not a simple decaying ellipsoid
  but has a nonlinear ridge along the (a_1, a_3) subspace,
  reflecting the Triad's harmonic locking.

  The funnel's output, at late times, is a single curve: the k=1
  mode amplitude decaying toward zero, with k=3 slaved to it at
  fixed ratio. This is the Stable Manifold Curve projected into
  modal space.

Where it appears in ED-Phys:
  - ED-Phys-29: complex initial conditions (step functions, noise,
    composites with k=4 and k=7 content) all converge to the same
    manifold as simple Gaussians. The funnel has done its work:
    higher modes have been erased.
  - ED-Phys-30: orthogonal probes (designed to excite non-fundamental
    modes) decay without affecting the convergence rate or manifold
    dimension. The funnel rejects orthogonal excitation.
  - ED-Phys-25, 26: k3_max is invariant across perturbations,
    confirming the Triad ridge within the funnel.

(h) THE NONLINEAR TRIAD LOCUS
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Name:       The Nonlinear Triad Locus
Definition: The region of physical space and state space where the
            M'(rho)|nabla(rho)|^2 term dominates the dynamics,
            generating higher-harmonic content through nonlinear
            mode coupling.

Governing laws:
  - Law of Harmonic Protection (the Triad's output is invariant
    under admissible perturbations).
  - Law of Modal Hierarchy (the Triad's output is enslaved to the
    fundamental).

Associated motifs:
  - Nonlinear Triad (the motif defined by this locus).
  - Saddle (provides the gradient geometry on which the Triad acts).

Geometric description:
  The Triad Locus exists in physical space wherever two conditions
  are simultaneously satisfied:
  (1) |nabla(rho)|^2 is large (steep density gradients).
  (2) M'(rho) is large (density is in the range where mobility
      changes rapidly).

  For the canonical mobility M(rho) = M0*(1 - rho/rho_max)^2:
    M'(rho) = -2*M0*(1 - rho/rho_max)/rho_max
  The magnitude |M'| is largest near rho = 0 and decreases toward
  rho_max. But |nabla(rho)|^2 is largest near the inflection points
  of the density profile, which are located on the flanks of the
  density peak.

  The Triad Locus is therefore geometrically located on the FLANKS
  of the density peak: the regions where the density is changing
  most rapidly in space AND where the mobility derivative is still
  significant. It is not at the peak itself (where nabla(rho) = 0)
  and not far from the peak (where the density is near rho* and
  gradients are small).

  The Triad Locus sits on the Saddle geometry: the Saddle motif
  creates the gradient structure, and the Triad acts on that
  structure to generate harmonics. The two motifs are spatially
  co-located and dynamically coupled.

  In the reduced phase space, the Triad Locus corresponds to the
  early-time portion of trajectories when density gradients are
  still large. As the system approaches equilibrium and gradients
  decay, the Triad's influence wanes. The Triad Locus shrinks
  toward zero as t increases, consistent with the Law of Transient
  Structure.

  The Triad's output amplitude k3_max scales with the initial
  amplitude: 0.54 at A=10, 2.17 at A=40, 3.52 at A=80 (ED-Phys-29).
  This scaling reflects the gradient dependence: larger initial
  amplitudes create steeper gradients, producing stronger Triad
  coupling. But at fixed amplitude, k3_max is invariant (< 0.01%
  variation), because the coupling geometry depends on the functional
  form of M'*|nabla(rho)|^2, which is architecturally protected.

Where it appears in ED-Phys:
  - ED-Phys-25: k3_max = 6.36, invariant across 5 penalty variants.
  - ED-Phys-26: k3_max = 6.44, invariant across 5 mobility variants.
  - ED-Phys-28: k3_max preserved under combined stress testing.
  - ED-Phys-29: k3_max scales with amplitude but not with IC type
    at fixed amplitude.


========================================================================
3. GEOMETRIC CONSEQUENCES OF THE LAWS
========================================================================

Each architectural law from ED-Arch-03 has a geometric consequence:
it determines or constrains the shape of one or more geometric objects.

LAW OF UNIQUE ATTRACTION -> SINGLE SINK GEOMETRY
  The entire phase space is a basin of attraction for a single point.
  There are no saddle points, no repellers, no limit cycles, no
  invariant tori. The flow field points inward everywhere. The global
  geometry is topologically equivalent to a ball with all flow lines
  converging to the center. This is the simplest possible phase
  portrait topology.

LAW OF ONE-DIMENSIONAL RELAXATION -> MANIFOLD CURVE
  The flow does not converge to the Attractor Point from all
  directions equally. It first collapses onto a one-dimensional
  curve (the Stable Manifold Curve) and then slides along that
  curve to the attractor. The geometric consequence is dimensional
  reduction: the infinite-dimensional state space is effectively
  replaced by a one-dimensional curve after the initial transient.
  The flow field has a strong contraction perpendicular to the
  manifold and a weak contraction along it.

LAW OF CRITICAL DAMPING BOUNDARY -> TOPOLOGICAL SURFACE
  The Boundary Surface D + 2*zeta = 1 divides parameter space into
  two topologically distinct regions. On one side, the Attractor
  Point is a focus (spiral approach). On the other, it is a node
  (monotonic approach). The surface is a codimension-1 object (a
  line in 2D parameter space, a plane in 3D). It is the geometric
  locus of a topological bifurcation in the flow type, though not
  a bifurcation in the number of attractors (which remains one
  throughout).

LAW OF FINITE CAPACITY -> HORIZON SURFACE
  The state space is bounded: rho cannot exceed rho_max at any
  spatial point. The Horizon Surface is the geometric boundary of
  the accessible region. It is a reflecting barrier, not an
  absorbing one. The penalty ensures that trajectories bouncing
  off the Horizon Surface are pushed back toward the interior.
  The geometric consequence is that the phase portrait is confined
  to a bounded region, preventing blowup.

LAW OF MODAL HIERARCHY -> FUNNEL GEOMETRY
  The k^2 decay hierarchy creates a funnel in modal amplitude space.
  The funnel's aspect ratio is extreme: the k=2 axis decays 4x
  faster than k=1, the k=3 axis 9x faster, and so on. This
  extreme aspect ratio is why the dimensional collapse to 1D is
  so rapid and robust. The geometric consequence is that even
  wildly different initial conditions (Gaussians, sines, steps,
  noise) all arrive at the same manifold curve within a few
  relaxation times of the fundamental mode.

LAW OF HARMONIC PROTECTION -> INVARIANT TRIAD LOCUS
  The Triad Locus maintains a fixed geometric relationship to the
  density peak and produces a fixed harmonic ratio (k3/k1)
  regardless of perturbations to M, P, or v. The geometric
  consequence is an invariant substructure within the funnel: the
  (a_1, a_3) ridge is architecturally fixed, not parameter-
  dependent. This invariant locus is the geometric fingerprint of
  the nonlinear coupling.

LAW OF PROPORTIONAL STABILITY -> GEOMETRIC INVARIANCE CLASS
  Multiplicative perturbations preserve the qualitative geometry
  of all objects: the Attractor Point remains a single point, the
  Manifold Curve remains one-dimensional, the Spiral Sheet retains
  its winding, the Boundary Surface remains linear. The geometric
  consequence is that the entire geometric structure is invariant
  under a large class of transformations. This defines an
  equivalence class of geometries: all PDE instances connected by
  multiplicative perturbations share the same qualitative geometry.

LAW OF CHANNEL COMPLEMENTARITY -> PARAMETER SPACE SIMPLEX
  The D + H = 1 constraint confines the architectural control
  to a one-dimensional simplex (the interval [0, 1] parameterized
  by D). The geometric consequence is that the effective parameter
  space is lower-dimensional than it first appears: (D, zeta, tau)
  is a three-dimensional space, but D alone controls the channel
  balance because H is not independent. The simplex structure
  means there is a natural ordering of cosmologies along a single
  axis.

LAW OF TRANSIENT STRUCTURE -> TEMPORAL SHRINKAGE
  All geometric objects other than the Attractor Point are transient:
  the Spiral Sheet, the Horizon Surface encounters, the Triad Locus,
  the Modal Funnel's cross-section all shrink toward zero as t
  increases. The geometric consequence is that the architecture's
  geometric richness is concentrated in the early-to-middle time
  evolution. At late times, only the Attractor Point and the final
  segment of the Manifold Curve remain.

4. GEOMETRIC INTERACTIONS
========================================================================

The geometric objects do not exist in isolation. They intersect,
constrain, contain, and transform each other. The following
interactions define the full geometric structure.

SPIRAL SHEET INTERSECTS STABLE MANIFOLD
  The Stable Manifold Curve is the central axis of the Spiral Sheet.
  Every spiral trajectory on the sheet asymptotically approaches the
  manifold curve, then follows it to the Attractor Point. The sheet
  wraps around the curve like a helix around a wire. As the spiral
  decays, the trajectory moves from the sheet's outer layers to its
  core (the manifold). The manifold is the sheet's skeleton.

HORIZON SURFACE INTERSECTS SPIRAL SHEET ONLY AT D = 0
  The Horizon Surface is a boundary in state space at high rho.
  The Spiral Sheet can reach this boundary only when the memory
  loop operates at full strength (H = 1, D = 0) and initial
  amplitude is large enough (A >= 40). At D >= 0.25, the diffusion
  channel dissipates the spiral's amplitude before it reaches the
  horizon. Geometrically, the Spiral Sheet's outer edge at D = 0
  grazes or penetrates the Horizon Surface, while at D >= 0.25,
  the sheet lies entirely interior to the horizon. The intersection,
  when it exists, is a curve on the Spiral Sheet where trajectories
  flatten temporarily.

BOUNDARY SURFACE CUTS THE SPIRAL SHEET'S DOMAIN
  The Boundary Surface D + 2*zeta = 1 is a plane in parameter
  space. The Spiral Sheet exists only on one side of this plane
  (D + 2*zeta < 1). The Monotonic Cone exists on the other side
  (D + 2*zeta > 1). At the plane itself, the Spiral Sheet's
  winding number drops to zero and the sheet degenerates into the
  Manifold Curve. The Boundary Surface is the Spiral Sheet's
  geometric death: the surface on which the sheet ceases to exist.

MODAL FUNNEL COLLAPSES INTO THE MANIFOLD CURVE
  The Modal Decay Funnel is a high-dimensional object (N-dimensional,
  where N is the number of Fourier modes) that narrows in time.
  Its output is the Manifold Curve: the one-dimensional curve that
  remains after all higher modes have decayed. The funnel's narrow
  end IS the manifold. The geometric interaction is one of
  containment and convergence: the funnel contains the manifold as
  its asymptotic limit.

NONLINEAR TRIAD LOCUS SITS ON THE SADDLE GEOMETRY
  The Saddle motif creates gradient structure at density extrema.
  The Triad Locus is located on the flanks of these extrema, where
  gradients are steepest. The two geometric objects are co-located
  in physical space: the Saddle provides the spatial geometry, and
  the Triad Locus is embedded within it. As the Saddle geometry
  weakens (density approaches equilibrium), the Triad Locus shrinks
  in proportion.

ATTRACTOR POINT SITS AT CENTER OF ALL OBJECTS
  Every geometric object is organized around the Attractor Point:
  - The Manifold Curve terminates at it.
  - The Spiral Sheet winds toward it.
  - The Monotonic Cone is centered on it.
  - The Horizon Surface faces it (the penalty points toward it).
  - The Modal Funnel narrows toward it.
  - The Boundary Surface divides the flow types approaching it.
  The Attractor Point is the geometric center of the architecture.

MONOTONIC CONE CONTAINS THE MANIFOLD CURVE (IN THE MONOTONIC REGIME)
  In the monotonic regime (D + 2*zeta > 1), the Manifold Curve is
  nearly straight and lies along the cone's axis. The cone contains
  all trajectories, and all trajectories converge to the manifold
  before sliding to the attractor. The cone's aperture determines
  how quickly trajectories find the manifold: narrow cones (high
  damping) produce fast manifold capture; wider cones (moderate
  damping) allow longer transients.

6. UNIFIED INTERPRETATION
========================================================================

WHAT THE GEOMETRY REVEALS ABOUT ED

The geometric structure of the ED cosmology PDE tells us that the
architecture is, at its core, a machine for dimensional reduction.
The system begins in an infinite-dimensional state space (arbitrary
density profiles over a spatial domain). The Modal Funnel collapses
this to a few effective dimensions within a few decay times. The
Manifold Curve further reduces it to one dimension. The Attractor
Point reduces it to zero.

The architecture's creativity — its oscillations, harmonics, horizons,
and transient complexity — all live in the geometry of this dimensional
collapse. The Spiral Sheet is the geometry of oscillatory collapse.
The Monotonic Cone is the geometry of direct collapse. The Horizon
Surface is the geometry of bounded collapse. The Triad Locus is the
geometry of structured collapse (collapse that generates new spectral
content along the way).

WHY GEOMETRY IS THE CORRECT ABSTRACTION ABOVE LAWS

Laws tell us what must be true. Geometry tells us what that looks like.
The Law of Unique Attraction says there is one equilibrium; the
Attractor Point shows us where it is and what the flow looks like
near it. The Law of Critical Damping Boundary says there is a
transition; the Boundary Surface shows us its shape and location.
The Law of Modal Hierarchy says higher modes decay faster; the Modal
Funnel shows us the shape of that decay.

Geometry is the natural language for describing the architecture's
global structure because:

  (1) Laws are local constraints. They tell us what happens at each
      point in parameter or state space. Geometry assembles these
      local constraints into global objects: surfaces, curves, regions.

  (2) Motifs are patterns. They describe what recurs. Geometry tells
      us where each pattern lives and how different patterns relate
      to each other spatially.

  (3) Behaviors are trajectories. Geometry provides the surfaces
      and channels along which those trajectories travel.

The geometric level of description completes the architectural
analysis because it makes the abstract concrete. The Spiral Sheet
is not a metaphor; it is a definite two-dimensional surface in the
parameter-augmented phase space with measurable curvature, definite
boundaries, and specific intersection properties. The Boundary
Surface is not an approximation; it is a plane with a known equation
(D + 2*zeta = 1) confirmed at every tested point.

HOW GEOMETRY COMPLETES THE ED-ARCH ONTOLOGY

The four ED-Arch documents form a complete structural description of
the unified ED cosmology PDE:

  ED-Arch-01 (Summary):    WHAT the architecture does.
  ED-Arch-02 (Motifs):     WHAT PATTERNS recur.
  ED-Arch-03 (Laws):       WHAT RULES govern.
  ED-Arch-04 (Geometry):   WHAT SHAPES result.

Geometry closes the loop. Laws constrain geometry. Geometry hosts
motifs. Motifs produce behaviors. Behaviors generate features.
Features confirm laws. The circle is complete.

The geometric picture also reveals the architecture's essential
simplicity. Despite the PDE's nonlinear complexity, the geometric
structure consists of only eight objects organized in a nested
hierarchy with a single center (the Attractor Point), a single
backbone (the Manifold Curve), a single partition (the Boundary
Surface), and a single outer boundary (the Horizon Surface). The
entire dynamical repertoire of the ED cosmology PDE — every
oscillation, every harmonic, every transient — lives within this
simple geometric scaffold.

The ED architecture, viewed geometrically, is a funnel that narrows
from infinite dimensions to one, split by a plane into spiral and
monotonic halves, bounded by a reflecting surface at maximum density,
and threaded by a one-dimensional curve along which all trajectories
converge to a single point. Everything else is transient decoration
on this skeleton. The geometry is the skeleton.
